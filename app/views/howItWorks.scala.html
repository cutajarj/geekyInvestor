What's all this fuss about?

The Geeky Investor helps you do your own market research. Instead of presenting you with a stock screener containing the usual numbers and charts, it allows you to enter expressions and work out exactly what you need. Building your expression is easy. Here is a cheat sheet that will tell you how to do that with some examples.

Devil's in the details.

Let's say you want to calculate the price earnings for a particular stock. That's easy right?
PE = Share Price / Earnings per share
Now let's say you would like to know how the company's PE did over the last 10 years. You start by getting a history of earnings for the last 10 years, which happen to be reported say every quarter. You can then apply the above expression on the day the report comes out. Unfortunately this would give you only 4 points a year, leaving you with huge gaps in between quarter reports.

Another way of looking at it is to use:
PE Today = Today's Price / Last Reported EPS
meaning that the last reported EPS is what is still valid and is replaced only when the next earnings become available. This is what most screeners (such as Yahoo! finance) and others report (hence why you see it changing every day). To build a historical series using this method you obtain daily stock prices and quarterly earnings. Then for every date you work out the PE by using the stock price and the latest earnings for that day.

This is exactly how the Geeky Investor works things out. Of course we also apply this to everything, not just PE. This proves to be useful in a lot of cases, for example when comparing fundamentals from companies reporting their earnings at different quarters.

What are your sources?

Data is fetched from various sources. Daily pricing is retrieved from APIs provided by Google and Yahoo! finance. Some fundamental data is also read from Yahoo!, while others are manually obtained from the respective company website. Currencies are retrieved using openexchangerates.org
